Why VO2max Is the Most Important Metric for Hybrid Athletes (And How to Actually Improve It)

Most fitness metrics are interesting. VO2max is useful. There's a difference, and for hybrid athletes specifically, it's a significant one.

[ Article Hero Image ]

VO2max — your body's maximum rate of oxygen consumption during exercise — is one of the few metrics that meaningfully predicts performance across both the strength and endurance domains you're training simultaneously. It's a number that tells you something real about your aerobic engine, your recovery capacity, and your long-term athletic potential.

This article covers what VO2max actually is, why it matters specifically for athletes who train both strength and endurance, and the two training methods that consistently move the number.

What VO2max Actually Measures

VO2max is measured in millilitres of oxygen per kilogram of bodyweight per minute (ml/kg/min). Untrained adults typically sit between 35 and 45. Competitive endurance athletes can reach 70 to 85. What the metric captures is how efficiently your cardiovascular system can deliver oxygen to working muscles, and how efficiently those muscles can use it.

A higher VO2max means a larger aerobic engine. That engine doesn't just matter for running. It matters for recovery between strength sets, for recovery between training days, and for how quickly your body can return to a state ready for the next training stimulus.

The number is influenced by cardiac output — how much blood your heart can pump per minute — stroke volume, the oxygen-carrying capacity of your blood, and the density of mitochondria in your muscles. These are all trainable. VO2max is not fixed.

Why It Matters Specifically for Hybrid Athletes

Pure strength athletes can get away with a modest aerobic base. Their sport doesn't demand it, and the interference effect — where endurance training can inhibit some strength adaptations — gives them a reasonable argument to deprioritise aerobic work.

Hybrid athletes don't have that option. You need the aerobic engine. The question is how to develop it without it interfering with your strength training — and that's where VO2max becomes the number worth paying attention to.

A higher VO2max gives you more headroom. It makes your easy runs feel easier, which means they're less stressful on your recovery. It improves your work capacity in strength sessions. And it accelerates recovery between sessions — which, when you're training both disciplines simultaneously, is one of the most valuable adaptations you can build.

The Two Training Methods That Actually Move the Needle

Not all aerobic training improves VO2max equally. The research is fairly settled on which methods consistently produce the most significant adaptations.

High-intensity interval training at or near VO2max pace. Typically intervals of 3 to 8 minutes at an intensity of around 90 to 95 percent of maximum heart rate, with equal or slightly shorter recovery periods. The research on HIIT for VO2max improvement is substantial and consistent. Repeated exposure to intensities that challenge your cardiovascular ceiling is what forces the ceiling upward. Two sessions per week is generally sufficient — more creates excessive fatigue without proportional return.

Zone 2 training. Steady-state aerobic work at a comfortable conversational pace, around 60 to 70 percent of maximum heart rate. Zone 2 doesn't feel like it's doing much. That's the point. The adaptations it drives are at the cellular level: mitochondrial density, fat oxidation efficiency, capillary development. These are the changes that give your aerobic engine its displacement. Interval work pushes the ceiling; Zone 2 builds the foundation beneath it.

For hybrid athletes, the practical protocol typically combines both: one or two higher-intensity sessions per week structured as proper intervals, plus whatever Zone 2 work fits naturally into your schedule as easy runs, cycling or rowing. The higher-intensity sessions are the productive constraint — the ones you plan and protect. Zone 2 fills the remaining aerobic volume without accumulating significant fatigue.

What This Means for Your Training Week

The scheduling challenge for hybrid athletes is fitting VO2max work into a week that already contains strength sessions, without triggering enough cumulative fatigue to undermine either.

The research on interference is fairly clear: the primary problem is residual fatigue, not some mystical physiological incompatibility between strength and endurance adaptation. If you're tired from a hard run when you attempt a strength session, the strength session suffers — not because your muscles can't adapt to both, but because you're compromised going in. The solution is sequencing, not elimination.

High-intensity aerobic work should not be scheduled immediately before a strength session that targets the same muscle groups. A hard interval run the morning before a heavy lower-body lift is a reliable way to compromise both. The reverse order — strength before cardio on the same day, if sessions must be stacked — is generally better because strength adaptations are more sensitive to pre-existing fatigue.

Two high-quality VO2max sessions per week, placed intelligently around your strength training, is enough to drive measurable improvement over an 8-week block. What matters is consistency of placement and quality of execution — not density of volume.
